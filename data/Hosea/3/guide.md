# 📖 Hosea Chapter 3 (KJV)

---

## 📌 Chapter at a Glance

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Total Verses** | 5 |
| **Type** | Narrative & Prophetic |
| **Key Theme** | Redemption, Restoration & Future Hope |
| **Central Action** | Hosea redeems Gomer — God redeems Israel |

> **Note:** Though the shortest chapter in Hosea — only **5 verses** — Chapter 3 is arguably the most **theologically concentrated** passage in the entire book. It is the **climax of the living parable** begun in Chapter 1 and the **personal heart** behind the restoration promises of Chapter 2.

### ✦ SECTION 1 — God's Command to Love Again (Verse 1)

> *"Then said the LORD unto me, Go yet, love a woman beloved of her friend, yet an adulteress, according to the love of the LORD toward the children of Israel, who look to other gods, and love flagons of wine."*
> — **Hosea 3:1 (KJV)**

---

#### 📖 Breaking Down Verse 1

| Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **"Go yet"** | God commands Hosea to **go again** — a second command of love toward the same unfaithful woman |
| **"Love a woman"** | The command is not merely to **take her back** but to actively **love** her — an act of will and grace, not feeling |
| **"Beloved of her friend"** | She has a lover — she is in the arms of another man — yet Hosea is commanded to love her still |
| **"Yet an adulteress"** | Her sinful condition is fully acknowledged — the command to love is given with full knowledge of her guilt |
| **"According to the love of the LORD"** | The **defining parallel** — Hosea's love for Gomer is to mirror **God's love for Israel** — this is the heartbeat of the entire book |
| **"Who look to other gods"** | Israel's active spiritual adultery — turning eyes and heart toward false gods |
| **"Love flagons of wine"** | Likely refers to **raisin cakes** used in Baal/idol feasts — Israel's attachment to the pleasures of idol worship (see Jeremiah 44:17–19) |

---

> **Key Insight:** The word **"yet"** (*again*) in verse 1 carries enormous weight. Gomer had already proven unfaithful. She had already left. She was now with another man. And God says to Hosea — **go AGAIN**. This is not naive love — it is **deliberate, costly, fully-informed grace**. God is saying to Israel through this act: *"I know everything you have done — and I am choosing to love you still."*

---

#### 🔍 The Parallel Stated Explicitly

| Hosea's Situation | God's Situation |
|---|---|
| Gomer — beloved yet adulteress | Israel — loved yet spiritually adulterous |
| Hosea commanded to love her again | God chooses to love Israel again |
| She is with another man | Israel looks to other gods |
| Love is not based on her worthiness | God's love is not based on Israel's faithfulness |

> *"According to the love of the LORD toward the children of Israel"*
> — **Hosea 3:1 (KJV)**

> This verse makes the **interpretation unmistakable** — God Himself draws the parallel. This is not merely a story about a broken marriage. This is **God's own love story with His people** — displayed in the flesh through Hosea and Gomer.

### ✦ SECTION 2 — Hosea Redeems Gomer (Verse 2)

> *"So I bought her to me for fifteen pieces of silver, and for an homer of barley, and an half homer of barley."*
> — **Hosea 3:2 (KJV)**

---

#### 📊 The Price of Redemption

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **"I bought her"** | A redemption price was paid — Gomer had to be **purchased** from her current situation |
| **Fifteen pieces of silver** | Half the standard price of a slave (Exodus 21:32 — full slave price = 30 pieces of silver) |
| **Homer and a half of barley** | A homer = approximately 6 bushels — roughly the other half of the slave price in grain |
| **Total Value** | Together the silver and barley likely equalled **30 pieces of silver** — the full price of a slave |
| **Significance of 30 pieces** | The standard redemption price — the price of a purchased/redeemed life |

---

#### 🔍 The Price — Deeper Significance

| Detail | Significance |
|---|---|
| **Gomer had to be bought** | She had fallen into such degradation that she was essentially in **slavery** — perhaps sold or indebted to a lover |
| **Hosea paid the full price** | He spared nothing to bring her back — **at personal cost** |
| **She contributed nothing** | Gomer did not pay her own way out — she was **entirely dependent on Hosea's payment** |
| **30 pieces of silver** | This is the same amount for which **Judas betrayed Christ** (Matthew 26:15) — yet here it is the price of **redemption**, not betrayal |
| **Gospel Parallel** | Christ paid the full price of redemption for sinners who could not pay for themselves (1 Peter 1:18–19) |

---

> **Key Insight:** Gomer did nothing to earn her redemption. She did not seek Hosea out. She did not repent her way back to him. **He went to her. He paid for her. He brought her home.** This is the very picture of **sovereign redeeming grace** — God seeking the lost before they seek Him, paying the price they could never pay.

---

### ✦ SECTION 3 — The Terms of Restoration (Verse 3)

> *"And I said unto her, Thou shalt abide for me many days; thou shalt not play the harlot, and thou shalt not be for another man: so will I also be for thee."*
> — **Hosea 3:3 (KJV)**

---

#### 📊 The Terms Hosea Sets Before Gomer

| Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **"Thou shalt abide for me many days"** | She is to remain — set apart, waiting — a period of discipline and probation begins |
| **"Thou shalt not play the harlot"** | All unfaithfulness must cease — absolute separation from her former life |
| **"Thou shalt not be for another man"** | Exclusive fidelity — she belongs to Hosea alone |
| **"So will I also be for thee"** | Hosea's reciprocal commitment — he also separates himself for her — faithful and waiting |

---

> **Key Insight:** This verse reveals the **tender yet firm** nature of restoration. Hosea does not immediately restore full marital relations — there is a **period of waiting and proving**. This is not rejection but **purposeful discipline** — a season designed to break the patterns of the past before full restoration is enjoyed. Gomer is redeemed but not yet fully restored — she is **His but the relationship is being rebuilt**.

---

#### 🔍 "So Will I Also Be For Thee"

> This phrase is one of the most beautiful in the chapter. Even during Gomer's period of discipline and waiting — **Hosea is also waiting for her**. He is not going elsewhere. He is not rejecting her. He is **faithful to the one he redeemed** while the restoration process unfolds. This mirrors God's own faithfulness to Israel even during the long centuries of discipline.

---

#### 🔗 Cross-References — Discipline Before Full Restoration

| Scripture | Connection |
|---|---|

| **Jeremiah 29:10–14** | *"After seventy years...I will turn away your captivity"* — a set period before restoration |
| **Hebrews 12:6–11** | *"Whom the Lord loveth he chasteneth"* — discipline is the mark of sonship, leading to righteousness |

### ✦ SECTION 4 — Israel's Prophetic Discipline (Verse 4)

> *"For the children of Israel shall abide many days without a king, and without a prince, and without a sacrifice, and without an image, and without an ephod, and without teraphim."*
> — **Hosea 3:4 (KJV)**

---

> **Key Insight:** Verse 4 is the **prophetic interpretation** of verse 3. Just as Gomer would abide **"many days"** in a state of discipline — neither fully restored nor in her old life — so Israel would abide **"many days"** stripped of every element of national and religious life.

---

#### 📊 The Six Things Israel Would Be Without

| # | What Removed | Category | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **A King** | Political | No sovereign ruler — loss of national government and monarchy |
| **2** | **A Prince** | Political | No civil leader — complete collapse of national leadership structure |
| **3** | **A Sacrifice** | Religious (True) | No Levitical system — the entire Mosaic sacrificial system ceases |
| **4** | **An Image** | Religious (False) | No idol worship — even the false religious system stripped away |
| **5** | **An Ephod** | Religious (True) | No priestly ministry — the high priestly garment and function gone |
| **6** | **Teraphim** | Religious (False) | No household gods or divination — all false religion removed |

---

#### 🔍 The Significance of Removing BOTH True and False Religion

> Notice that God removes **both the true worship system** (sacrifice, ephod) **and the false** (image, teraphim). Israel would be left with **neither** during the long period of exile and discipline.

| Removed | Type | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Sacrifice & Ephod** | True Religion | No access to the Levitical system — no temple, no priesthood |
| **Image & Teraphim** | False Religion | No idols — even the false comfort of idol worship taken away |

> **Key Insight:** This is **total stripping** — God removes every religious crutch, true and false alike. Israel would be left with nothing but the bare reality of their condition before God. The purpose is not permanent abandonment but to bring them to the place where **God alone** is their hope.

---

#### 📊 Historical Fulfillment of Hosea 3:4

| Element Removed | Historical Fulfillment |
|---|---|
| **King & Prince** | Northern Israel — 722 BC Assyrian conquest; Judah — 586 BC Babylonian conquest; no Davidic king since 586 BC |
| **Sacrifice** | Temple destroyed — 586 BC (Babylon); again 70 AD (Rome) — sacrifice has not been offered since 70 AD |
| **Ephod** | Priestly ministry ceased with destruction of temple and priesthood |
| **Image & Teraphim** | Idolatry largely purged from Israel after the Babylonian captivity |
| **"Many days"** | Israel has been without these things for **over 2,500 years** — still ongoing |

> **Key Insight:** This is one of the most remarkably **fulfilled prophecies** in Scripture. Israel has indeed lived **"many days"** — centuries — without a king, without a functioning priestly system, without a temple sacrifice. The **"many days"** of Hosea 3:4 stretches from 722 BC to the present day — a period of over 2,700 years.

### ✦ SECTION 5 — Israel's Future Return (Verse 5) ⭐ *Key Verse*

> *"Afterward shall the children of Israel return, and seek the LORD their God, and David their king; and shall fear the LORD and his goodness in the latter days."*
> — **Hosea 3:5 (KJV)**

---

#### 📊 The Five Elements of Israel's Future Restoration

| Element | Verse | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **"Afterward"** | v.5 | After the long discipline of v.4 — there is a determined **divine timetable** |
| **"Shall return"** | v.5 | A genuine, national turning back — the prodigal nation comes home |
| **"Seek the LORD their God"** | v.5 | Active, earnest pursuit of God — not passive drift but deliberate seeking |
| **"David their king"** | v.5 | The Messiah — the Son of David — the ultimate King of Israel |
| **"Fear the LORD and his goodness"** | v.5 | Reverent awe combined with joyful wonder at God's goodness — the perfect response |
| **"In the latter days"** | v.5 | The eschatological future — the end times fulfillment of God's purposes for Israel |

---

#### 🔍 "David Their King" — The Messianic Promise

> The phrase **"David their king"** is one of the most significant Messianic statements in the Minor Prophets.

| Interpretation | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Not a resurrected David** | David himself does not return as king |
| **The Son of David** | Points to the **Messiah** — born of David's line (see Matthew 1:1; Luke 1:32–33) |
| **The One King** | Connects to Hosea 1:11 — *"appoint themselves one head"* |
| **Eternal King** | The Davidic Covenant (2 Samuel 7:12–16) promises an eternal throne — fulfilled in Christ |
| **Cross-References** | Ezekiel 34:23–24; 37:24–25; Jeremiah 30:9 — all use "David" as a title for the coming Messianic King |

---

#### 📊 "David Their King" — Parallel Prophecies

| Scripture | Wording | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Hosea 3:5** | *"David their king"* | Israel returns to the Son of David |
| **Jeremiah 30:9** | *"David their king, whom I will raise up unto them"* | God raises up the Davidic Messiah |
| **Ezekiel 34:23** | *"My servant David...he shall feed them"* | The Good Shepherd — Messianic King |
| **Ezekiel 37:24** | *"David my servant shall be king over them"* | One king over reunited Israel |
| **Luke 1:32–33** | *"The throne of his father David...his kingdom no end"* | Jesus — the fulfillment |

---

#### 🔍 "Fear the LORD and His Goodness"

> This phrase is beautifully balanced — combining two aspects of true worship:

| Element | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **"Fear the LORD"** | Reverent awe — the holy trembling before God's majesty and holiness |
| **"His goodness"** | Wonder and joy at the gracious, compassionate character of God |

> **Key Insight:** The restored Israel will **fear God not because of terror but because of goodness**. It is God's **goodness** that will overwhelm them — the realisation that this God who had every right to abandon them **chose to redeem them**. This is the highest form of holy fear — the fear that flows from encountering grace, not wrath.

---

#### 📊 "In the Latter Days" — Eschatological Fulfillment

| View | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Partial Fulfillment** | Some Jews returned from Babylon (538 BC) and sought God under Ezra & Nehemiah |
| **Progressive Fulfillment** | The Church age — Gentiles (spiritual Israel) seeking God through Christ |
| **Future Fulfillment** | The ultimate national restoration of Israel at the Second Coming of Christ |
| **"Latter Days"** | A consistently eschatological phrase in the OT pointing to the **Messianic age and end times** |

## 🕊️ Key Spiritual Lessons from Hosea 3

| Lesson | Verse |
|---|---|
| **God's love is unconditional and initiating** — He commands love toward the undeserving before any sign of repentance | v.1 |
| **Redemption always costs the redeemer** — Hosea paid a price; Christ paid infinitely more | v.2 |
| **The redeemed are called to a new life** — bought back for a purpose, not to return to the old ways | v.3 |
| **God's discipline is purposeful and temporary** — the "many days" has an "afterward" | v.4–5 |
| **The Messiah is the centre of all restoration** — Israel's return is ultimately to "David their king" | v.5 |
| **True worship combines holy fear with joyful wonder** — fearing the LORD **and** His goodness | v.5 |
| **God's covenant purposes cannot be permanently frustrated** — "afterward shall the children of Israel return" | v.5 |
